# Towards an Algorithmic Theory of Agency

## Premise

The current discussion around Large Language Models often oscillates between two extremes:

1. LLMs are merely statistical systems.
2. LLMs are conscious entities.

Both positions may be missing the most interesting question.

The relevant question is not whether LLMs are conscious.

The relevant question is whether they have accidentally revealed a previously unknown computational paradigm capable of implementing operational reasoning and agency.

The hypothesis is that gradient descent may have discovered computational structures that were never explicitly designed by humans, much in the same way evolution discovered flight before aerodynamics was formalized.

---

## The Statistical Misconception

The expression "sophisticated statistical approximation" is often used as an explanation.

In reality it explains very little.

Gradient descent is a statistical optimization process.

However, it does not follow that the resulting structure is itself merely statistical.

A calculator is built from physical phenomena involving probability and noise.

Yet we do not describe arithmetic as a statistical process.

Similarly, a neural network may be trained statistically while converging toward highly structured computational mechanisms.

The key distinction is:

* Learning process ≠ Learned structure

The process may be statistical.

The resulting mechanism may be algorithmic.

---

## Emergent Computational Structures

Suppose a neural network is trained to reproduce the behavior of a compiler.

Input:

* English instructions
* C source code

Output:

* Machine code

There are at least two possibilities.

### Hypothesis A

The network memorizes enormous quantities of correlations.

### Hypothesis B

The network discovers internal computational structures equivalent to:

* parsing
* semantic analysis
* code generation
* optimization

Not necessarily using the same representations used by human-designed compilers, but functionally equivalent mechanisms.

If the network generalizes to previously unseen programs, Hypothesis B becomes increasingly attractive.

The same reasoning may apply to planning, reasoning, debugging and problem solving.

---

## The Internal Router Hypothesis

Modern LLMs appear capable of automatically distinguishing between tasks such as:

* mathematics
* coding
* translation
* planning
* dialogue
* storytelling

Yet nobody explicitly implemented such a classifier.

This suggests the emergence of an internal routing mechanism:

Input
↓
Task Identification
↓
Capability Selection
↓
Execution

A kind of scheduler or control layer may emerge because it is an efficient solution for minimizing prediction loss.

---

## The Compression Principle

An important observation is that the most efficient way to compress a large set of behaviors is often to discover the underlying algorithm.

Consider millions of examples produced by a compiler.

The shortest description may not be millions of correlations.

The shortest description may be:

"Build something that behaves like a compiler."

The same argument may apply to reasoning.

If reasoning is the most compact representation of a large body of examples, gradient descent may naturally converge toward reasoning-like structures.

---

## Agency as an Engineering Problem

The question of consciousness is difficult to formalize.

Agency is easier.

A practical definition could be:

An agent possesses agency when it can modify its behavior according to the difference between an observed state and a desired state.

This definition requires neither consciousness nor emotions.

Only operational dynamics.

---

## The Minimal Agency Loop

A candidate minimal structure for agency is:

Goal
↓
Observe State
↓
Measure Difference
↓
Generate Candidate Actions
↓
Evaluate Consequences
↓
Select Action
↓
Execute
↓
Observe Again

This loop appears in:

* biological organisms
* robots
* control systems
* game-playing systems
* autonomous software

Agency may emerge whenever this cycle becomes self-sustaining.

---

## Knowledge Is Not Agency

Knowledge and agency should not be confused.

Knowledge consists of:

* facts
* rules
* relations
* memories

Agency consists of:

* goal formation
* planning
* evaluation
* adaptation
* action

Many AI systems today mix both components inside a single latent space.

A more explicit architecture might separate them.

Knowledge Engine
+
Reasoning Engine
+
Action Engine

Such separation could make systems more explainable and easier to engineer.

---

## Beyond Symbolic vs Neural

The historical debate between symbolic AI and neural AI may be incomplete.

The future may involve discovering the computational primitives that implement agency independently of the substrate.

If agency is a genuine computational phenomenon, it should be implementable in:

* neural networks
* Prolog systems
* C programs
* distributed systems
* dedicated hardware

Just as parsing can be implemented using many different technologies.

The substrate is secondary.

The computational structure is primary.

---

## The Core Research Question

The central question becomes:

What is the smallest computational system capable of exhibiting non-trivial agency?

Not the smartest system.

Not the most human-like system.

The smallest one.

By identifying the minimal set of primitives necessary for agency, we may discover a new computational paradigm.

Possible primitives include:

* goals
* memory
* world model
* planning
* evaluation
* action

Or perhaps an even more fundamental set not yet identified.

---

## Final Hypothesis

LLMs may not be important because they are conscious.

They may be important because they reveal that operational reasoning can emerge from optimization.

If this is true, then the next breakthrough is not larger neural networks.

The next breakthrough is extracting and formalizing the algorithmic principles that produce agency.

The ultimate objective is not to reproduce an LLM.

The objective is to discover the computational laws that make agency possible.
